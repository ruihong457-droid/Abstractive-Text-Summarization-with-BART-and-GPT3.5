 - The speaker is a health psychologist who previously taught that stress is harmful but has changed their perspective based on new research.
- A major study tracked 30,000 U.S. adults over 8 years, assessing stress levels, beliefs about stress, and mortality.
- Key finding: High stress increased risk of death by 43% only in people who believed stress was harmful.
- People with high stress who did not view stress as harmful had the lowest risk of dying, even lower than those with little stress.
- Believing stress is bad for you may cause over 20,000 premature deaths annually in the U.S., making it a significant cause of death.
- Changing how you think about stress can alter your body's physical response to it.
- In a Harvard study, participants taught to view stress responses (e.g., pounding heart, faster breathing) as helpful showed healthier cardiovascular responses and felt less anxious and more confident.
- Viewing stress positively can lead to a cardiovascular profile similar to moments of joy and courage, potentially reducing stress-related heart disease.
- The speaker’s goal shifted from eliminating stress to helping people manage stress better.
- Stress also triggers the release of oxytocin, a hormone that promotes social bonding, empathy, and support-seeking behavior.
- Oxytocin acts as a natural anti-inflammatory, relaxes blood vessels, and helps the heart heal from stress-induced damage.
- Social connection during stress increases oxytocin release, making the stress response healthier and aiding faster recovery.
- Another study of 1,000 adults found that while stressful life events increased mortality risk by 30%, those who spent time helping others showed no increase in stress-related death risk.
- Caring for others creates resilience against the harmful effects of stress.
- How you think about and act during stress can transform its impact on your health.
- Viewing stress as helpful creates "the biology of courage."
- Connecting with others under stress builds resilience.
- Stress can be a source of meaning, compassion, and strength if approached with the right mindset.
- Advice: Pursue meaningful activities and trust yourself to handle the stress that comes with them rather than avoiding stress altogether.
